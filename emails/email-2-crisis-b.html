The Salvation Army San Francisco

In 2024, The Way Out served 1,060 people in San Francisco. 80% of them arrived homeless. By the time they exited the program, 77% were self-sufficient — employed, housed, and no longer dependent on public services. That's not a soft outcome. It's a measurable system shift.

San Francisco's homelessness crisis is not a resource shortage. The city spends more per unhoused resident than almost any municipality in the country. The shortage is in models that actually work. Most interventions address symptoms — emergency shelter, short-term services — without changing the underlying trajectory of addiction, unemployment, and housing instability that drives chronic homelessness.

The Way Out works because it addresses all three simultaneously. Participants receive addiction treatment, stable housing, and workforce development inside a single integrated program — not three separate agencies with disconnected goals and no accountability for shared outcomes.

In 2024, Way Out participants collectively earned $473,000 in income and paid $138,000 in taxes — economic reintegration at scale, not just individual recovery.
