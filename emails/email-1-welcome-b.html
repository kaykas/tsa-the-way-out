THE WAY OUT

The Salvation Army San Francisco

San Francisco spends $60,000–$100,000 per person per year on homelessness — and the population remains near its all-time high. The Way Out — California's first Recovery System of Care — operates a full system of care for $31,000 per person, with a 77% success rate at program exit.

That gap isn't a rounding error. It's the difference between spending to manage a crisis and spending to end it. The Way Out — run by The Salvation Army in San Francisco — is built around outcomes: treatment on demand, recovery housing, job training, and structured long-term support. Every component is designed to move people from dependency to self-sufficiency.

Most public spending addresses immediate need without changing the underlying trajectory. The Way Out is different because it measures the exit, not just the entry. In 2024, 80% of participants entered the program homeless. 77% left self-sufficient.

$31,000 per person. 77% self-sufficient at exit. 46% residential completion rate vs. 12% city average. 1,060 served in 2024. That's the Way Out model in four numbers.

Over the next few weeks, we'll walk you through exactly how that model works — the components, the cost structure, the outcomes data, and why this approach delivers results that most homelessness programs don't.
