The $31,000 cost per participant in The Way Out isn't a flat fee. It's a sequence of services that, taken together, produce a 77% self-sufficiency rate. Understanding what each component does — and why the order matters — explains why integrated programs outperform single-service interventions.

Entry is immediate and barrier-free. Participants go directly into medically supervised treatment without waitlists. Stable housing follows on Day 1 — not as a reward for sobriety, but as a precondition for it. Once housed and in treatment, participants begin workforce development: resume support, vocational training, employer connections, and job placement assistance. Throughout, case managers track individual progress and adjust plans when people stall or backslide.

Consider what that sequence looked like for one participant — call him Jose. Entered homeless, unemployed, and in active addiction. Week 1: treatment and housing. Month 2: culinary training program through The Way Out's workforce partnerships. Month 6: placed in a kitchen role with a participating employer. Month 12: promoted to line lead, apartment leased independently. That trajectory — replicated across hundreds of participants annually — is what the data reflects.

Programs that address only one dimension — only housing, or only treatment — consistently underperform. The Way Out's outcomes data reflects what happens when all three are integrated inside a single accountable system.
