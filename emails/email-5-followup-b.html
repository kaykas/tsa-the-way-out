THE WAY OUT

The Salvation Army San Francisco

The Salvation Army has operated in San Francisco since 1883 — through the 1906 earthquake, the Depression, the AIDS crisis, and every iteration of the city's homelessness emergency. The Way Out — California's first Recovery System of Care — is the current expression of that 140-year track record: a data-driven, outcomes-focused system built on what actually works, not what's politically convenient or easy to fund.

Over the last month, you've seen the numbers: 1,060 served. 77% self-sufficient at exit. $31,000 per person — less than half the city's per-capita spend. $473,000 in participant income generated. $138,000 in taxes paid back into the system. These aren't projections or estimates. They're 2024 program outcomes from a system that has operated continuously for over a century.

Longevity matters in social services because it signals what metrics can't fully capture: organizational credibility, community trust, and the ability to retain the staff and relationships that make integrated care possible. Programs that work for a few years and then collapse leave gaps. The Way Out has never left a gap.
