THE WAY OUT

The Salvation Army San Francisco

San Francisco's homelessness budget now exceeds $700 million annually — roughly $70,000 per unhoused resident per year. That spending funds emergency services, outreach, shelter beds, and transitional programs. What it has not produced is a measurable decline in the city's chronic homeless population.

The Way Out costs $31,000 per person for a full program year. That includes medically supervised addiction treatment, stable housing, workforce development, case management, and post-exit support. The 77% self-sufficiency rate at program exit means that for roughly $31,000, a participant moves off public services, into employment, and into stable independent housing — and stays there.

The math is straightforward. A person who exits The Way Out self-sufficient is no longer consuming emergency services, shelter beds, or crisis intervention at $70,000 per year. They are, instead, employed and taxpaying — as the 2024 cohort demonstrated, generating $473,000 in collective income and $138,000 in taxes paid. Donor capital invested in The Way Out doesn't just help individuals. It changes the long-term cost curve for the city.

$70,000/year with no outcome benchmark vs. $31,000 with a 77% self-sufficiency rate. The Way Out is not just more effective — it is 55% less expensive per person served.

Private donors are what make this possible. City contracts don't fully fund integrated care at this level. Your support is the difference between a program that proves what works and one that can't reach enough people to matter.
