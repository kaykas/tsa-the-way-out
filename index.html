Step 1
Crisis Entry

The Way Out begins with one principle: when someone is ready to get help, they get help that day. No waitlist. No prerequisites. Because the window of willingness is narrow - we don't waste it.

Crisis entry covers two phases: Treatment on Demand (immediate access to care) and Detox & Stabilization (medically supported detox and a safe place to land). Both happen before a person is ready for residential treatment.

Facilities at this stage:

  • Harbor Light Wellness Center
  • Hope House, Eleanore Fagan Center

"I'd tried to get help before. I was told to come back in two weeks. Harbor Light took me the same day I walked in. That was the difference between me being here and not being here."

- Way Out participant, 3 years in recovery FPO

Step 2
Treatment & Recovery

Residential treatment provides structured, long-term recovery programming: therapy, peer support, skill-building, and accountability. This is the core of The Way Out - a proven model that treats addiction as a health condition, not a character flaw.

Facilities at this stage:

  • Harbor Light Residential - long-term residential recovery for men
  • Pinehurst Women's Recovery - programming designed around the distinct challenges women face, including trauma and family reunification

"Pinehurst gave me time to actually heal - not just stop using, but understand why I started. I've been clean for two years. My daughter calls me every Sunday."

- Pinehurst Women's Recovery graduate FPO
Support The Way Out
Step 3
Housing & Work

Recovery without stability doesn't last. This stage provides a roof, a structure, a community, and a path to income - so that when participants leave, they have somewhere to go and a way to support themselves.

Facilities at this stage:

  • Joseph McFee Center - transitional recovery housing
  • Wells Place - recovery housing
  • Marina Recovery Housing - community-based recovery housing
  • Culinary Training Academy - industry credentials, externships, and job placement in San Francisco restaurants
  • Railton Place & James Baldwin Place (optional) - permanent supportive housing for those who need more time before full independence

"I got my ServSafe certification at the Culinary Academy. I'm a line cook now in the Mission. I have a bank account. I have a routine. I have a life. Six months ago I was sleeping outside."

- Culinary Training Academy graduate FPO

Step 4
Independence

Recovery doesn't end at program exit. The Alumni Support Network keeps graduates connected - peer mentorship, community events, and access to resources when life gets hard. This ongoing connection is a core reason the 77% success rate holds.

Graduates also become the most powerful voices in the building: living proof that The Way Out works, and advocates for the people still in it.

"I went back to volunteer at Harbor Light last year. Standing in that room, knowing where I started - that's the whole story. I wanted someone else to see what I saw: that there's a way through this."

- Alumni Support Network member, 4 years independent FPO

Why The Salvation Army

The Salvation Army has served San Francisco's poor and unhoused since 1883. We're one of the city's largest nonprofit landowners. We don't just run programs - we own the infrastructure.

The Way Out isn't a pilot. It's a scaled system with a track record, a recovery model, and real results donors can measure. The Salvation Army has owned and operated this infrastructure since 1883 — we don't just run programs, we sustain them.

A full system of care. Not a shelter. Not a hand-out.

The Way Out — California's first Recovery System of Care — is The Salvation Army's recovery-focused initiative for San Francisco's homeless and addicted population. We don't offer a bed and a wish. We offer a proven path - from detox to employment, from crisis to self-sufficiency.

  • Treatment on Demand with real-time access to detox and stabilization
  • Two to four years of recovery housing paired with workforce development
  • Culinary Training Academy with credentials, placement, and a career path
  • Alumni Support Network because recovery doesn't end at program exit
